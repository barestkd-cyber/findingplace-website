A Reggio Emilia-Inspired School in Tyler, Texas

We take inspiration from the Reggio Emilia approach: the belief that every child is capable, curious, and full of potential, and that good teaching follows the child.

Families exploring early learning options in Tyler often come across the Reggio Emilia approach and wonder what it actually means for a child's day. At The Finding Place, it is the heart of how we teach. Here is what it looks like.

Where the Reggio Approach Comes From

The Reggio Emilia approach was born in the town of Reggio Emilia, Italy, in the years after World War II, shaped by the educator Loris Malaguzzi and a community of parents who wanted something better for their children. Its central belief is simple and powerful: every child is capable, curious, and full of potential from birth. The adult's job is not to fill the child up, but to listen closely, follow the child, and create the conditions for real discovery.

The Child as a Capable Protagonist

In a Reggio-inspired classroom, children are not passive recipients of lessons. They are active investigators of their own world. We watch what genuinely captures a child's attention and then build on it. When a child becomes fascinated by the way water moves, or how a spider builds a web, that fascination becomes the starting point for learning that is rich in language, math, science, and art.

The Hundred Languages of Children

Malaguzzi described the "hundred languages of children," the idea that young children express what they understand in countless ways, not only with words. A child might show what they know by building, painting, sculpting, moving, telling a story, or acting it out. We give children the materials, the time, and the freedom to use all of these languages, because a four-year-old usually understands far more than they can yet say.

The Environment as the Third Teacher

Reggio educators speak of the environment as the "third teacher," after the adults and the other children. We take that seriously, and for us the environment includes the outdoors. Our space is calm, beautiful, and full of natural materials, and the East Texas garden and outdoors serve as a living classroom every single day. You can read more about that on our nature school page.

Following the Child: Learning That Emerges

Rather than marching through a fixed curriculum, we let much of our learning emerge from the children's real interests, explored over days or weeks as projects. This is how children build deep focus, persistence, and a genuine love of learning, the kind that lasts long after they leave us.

"Nothing without joy." This belief of Loris Malaguzzi guides every part of our day.

Why "Inspired," and Why Small

We say Reggio-inspired rather than Reggio because the original approach belongs to its community in Italy, and we honor it by adapting its principles thoughtfully rather than claiming a label we have not earned. Because The Finding Place is a micro school with only a handful of children, we can do what the Reggio approach truly asks: know each child deeply, follow their interests, and document their growth. That intimacy is our greatest strength.
